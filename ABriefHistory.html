New Orleans Education

This site strives to focus on sites of schooling in New Orleans, primarily those that were attended by and continue to enroll Black New Orleanians. I use the term “Black New Orleanians” to include Black New Orleanians and Black Creole New Orleanians, as they self-identify. This distinction is important because not all schools catered and supported all Black students, though the brevity of this project does not untangle such tensions. This is best seen in the choice to include both Catholic and public schools in New Orleans.

Below, I offer a the beginning to a brief summary of New Orleans History in relation to education. Soon to come is a visual timeline, along with more detail about distinctive eras.

While education did not "begin" in New Orleans during the colonial period, as settler colonialists brought with them very distinctive forms of education, currlculum, and pedagogies, it is important to discuss education in the colonial period. In 1841, Mayor William Freret signed Ordinance No. 159, authorizing public schools in the Second Municipality (parts of the city above the canal) of New Orleans and initiate public funding for “all children of proper age, of white resident parents”. Though it was still illegal to education slaves, about a fifth of the city’s population consisted of Free People of Color in New Orleans, who paid taxes to a public resource they could not access. Though wealthy Free People of Color were able to send their male children to France, public schools were not legally accessible until the Freedman’s Bureau schools during the “Reconstruction” era.
