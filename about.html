About Mapping Sites of Memory

  1. Home
  2. About

Hurricane Katrina continues to impact the ways of life in the Gulf Region, including New Orleans, Louisiana. Following the environmental landfall of Hurricane Katrina, and now through continued sociopolitical acts, New Orleans receives a great amount of attention in education research.

Scholars have discussed the growth of charter schools (Dixson, Buras, & Jeffers, 2015), traditional versus alternative teaching programs (Buras, 2011), the impact of standardized testing (Ladson-Billings, 2007). This work is very necessary and has uncovered the complex layers and viewpoints of those impacted by such issues. This study, however, seeks to attend to the nuances of place, race, and schooling by bringing forth the importance of suspending damage-centered research (Tuck, 2009). Rhetoric about the city of New Orleans often situates it as a place for joy and celebration, or a place for sin and hopelessness. Tuck (2009) argues that “in a damage-centered framework, pain, and loss are documented in order to obtain particular political or material gains” (p. 413). And much research about New Orleans, its people, and education does just that by centering pain, loss, and hopelessness. New Orleanians are always “recovering”.

Building upon spatializing literacies (Leander & Sheehy, 2004), critical place inquiry (Tuck & McKenzie, 2015), Black feminist geographies (McKittrick, 2006), and the digital humanities, this project considers intersections of spatiality, race, literature, and education by analyzing the sociohistorical discourse about New Orleans education from 1920-Present. This projects seeks to conceptualize and visualize the temporal, spatial, and discourse around race and schooling through the digital humanities.

This phase of the digital project maps various educational spaces across the city of New Orleans from 1920s-Present-Day, almost a century. The next phase is to map these sites through photographs, scholarship, and oral histories, this will present “present-day” s/places, even those that appear erased, alongside other temporal mappings of the city. This phase of the project primarily focuses on the weighted narrative of schooling for New Orleanians of Color, especially during an era in which New Orleans public schools are considered “ground-zero”.

Because many New Orleanians lost documents and texts, such as photographs, recordings, yearbooks, etc., New Orleans residents (past, present, and always) will have a chance to share their memories of their schooling. This project comes at a time when New Orleans is still experiencing gentrification through systemic removal, following the narrative of Hurricane Katrina wiping the city of its previous life. Rather than serving to add to a rhetoric of a (re)new(ed) New Orleans, this project works as a digital spatial rememory and community. It also serves as an opportunity to celebrate the work of youth emerging from these spaces.
